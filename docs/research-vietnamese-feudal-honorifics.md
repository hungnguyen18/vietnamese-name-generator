## 1. Royal Court Titles (Triều dinh)

### Emperor Titles

| Title | Meaning | Usage |
|-------|---------|-------|
| **Hoang de** (皇帝) | Emperor | Full sovereign title claiming equality with China. First used by Dinh Bo Linh (968). Used domestically by most dynasties. |
| **Quoc vuong** (國王) | King of the State | Title used in diplomatic relations with China, where Vietnamese rulers accepted vassal status. Also used internally by some early rulers. |
| **Thien tu** (天子) | Son of Heaven | Ceremonial/ritual title borrowed from Chinese tradition. Rarely used independently; typically paired with Hoang de. |
| **Vua** | Ruler/King (native Vietnamese) | Colloquial term used by common people. Not a formal court title but the most widely used word for the monarch. |

**Key distinction:** Vietnamese rulers used Hoang de internally to assert sovereignty, while accepting the lesser title Quoc vuong in correspondence with China. The Nguyen dynasty (1802-1945) formally used Hoang de after Gia Long unified the country.

### Empress and Consort Hierarchy

**Top tier (above the nine ranks):**

| Title | Role |
|-------|------|
| **Hoang hau** (皇后) | Empress, principal wife of the emperor |
| **Hoang Thai hau** (皇太后) | Empress Dowager, mother of the reigning emperor |
| **Thai Hoang Thai hau** (太皇太后) | Grand Empress Dowager, grandmother of the reigning emperor |
| **Hoang Quy phi** (皇貴妃) | Imperial Noble Consort, rank directly below Empress. Established by Minh Mang in 1836. |

**Nine Ranks of Consorts (Cuu giai phi tan) -- Nguyen Dynasty:**

| Rank | Title | Approximate Translation |
|------|-------|------------------------|
| 1st (Nhat giai) | **Phi** (妃) | Royal Consort |
| 2nd (Nhi giai) | **Phi** | Royal Consort (lower) |
| 3rd (Tam giai) | **Tan** (嬪) | Imperial Concubine |
| 4th (Tu giai) | **Tan** | Imperial Concubine (lower) |
| 5th (Ngu giai) | **Tan** | Imperial Concubine (lower) |
| 6th (Luc giai) | **Tiep du** (婕妤) | Handmaiden |
| 7th (That giai) | **Quy nhan** (貴人) | Noble Person |
| 8th (Bat giai) | **My nhan** (美人) | Beautiful Person |
| 9th (Cuu giai) | **Tai nhan** (才人) | Talented Person |

Within Phi ranks, specific epithets were bestowed: **Quy phi** (Noble Consort), **Thuc phi** (Virtuous Consort), **Duc phi** (Gracious Consort), **Hien phi** (Worthy Consort), **Trang phi**, etc. These epithets expressed the emperor's personal regard and differentiated consorts of the same rank.

**Note:** The Nguyen system differed from China's Qing model. Titles were more fixed by rank rather than individually bestowed, though epithets within ranks did vary. Earlier dynasties (Ly, Tran, Le) had simpler harem structures; the elaborate nine-rank system was primarily a Nguyen dynasty codification.

### Princes and Princesses

**Princes (male imperial family):**

| Title | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **Thai tu** (太子) | Crown Prince, heir apparent designated by the emperor |
| **Hoang tu** (皇子) | Imperial Prince, son of the emperor (generic) |
| **Than vuong** (親王) | Prince of the Blood, highest princely rank for emperor's sons/brothers |
| **Quan vuong** (郡王) | Commandery Prince, lower princely rank |

**Princesses (female imperial family):**

| Title | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **Cong chua** (公主) | Princess, daughter of the emperor |
| **Quan chua** (郡主) | Commandery Princess, granddaughter of the emperor or daughter of a Than vuong |
| **Huyen chua** (縣主) | County Princess, lower-ranking princess, typically daughter of a Quan vuong |

### Nobility Ranks (Tuoc vi)

The feudal Vietnamese peerage system followed the Chinese "Five Ranks of Nobility" (Ngu dang tuoc) plus the royal rank of Vuong:

| Rank | Title | Translation |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1 | **Vuong** (王) | Prince/King (reserved for imperial blood) |
| 2 | **Cong** (公) | Duke -- subdivided into Quoc cong and Quan cong |
| 3 | **Hau** (侯) | Marquis |
| 4 | **Ba** (伯) | Count/Earl |
| 5 | **Tu** (子) | Viscount |
| 6 | **Nam** (男) | Baron |

The Le dynasty formalized "Cong, Hau, Ba, Tu, Nam" in 1471 under Le Thanh Tong. The Nguyen dynasty further subdivided Cong into Quoc cong (National Duke) and Quan cong (Commandery Duke), and added Than cong (Prince-Duke) for close imperial relatives.

**Dynastic variations:** The 11-rank system mentioned in some sources counts subdivisions (e.g., separating Than vuong, Quan vuong, Quoc cong, Quan cong as distinct ranks plus the five standard ranks plus additional female titles).

## 2. Mandarin/Official System (Quan lai)

### The Nine-Rank System (Cuu pham)

Each of the nine ranks was subdivided into Senior (Chanh) and Junior (Tong), yielding 18 grades total. Both Civil (Van) and Military (Vo) officials held ranks within this system.

| Rank | Senior (Chanh) | Junior (Tong) | Key Civil Positions | Key Military Positions |
|------|----------------|---------------|---------------------|----------------------|
| 1st | Chanh Nhat pham | Tong Nhat pham | Grand Chancellor, Grand Preceptor | Grand Marshal |
| 2nd | Chanh Nhi pham | Tong Nhi pham | **Thuong thu** (Minister of a Board) | Provincial Military Commander |
| 3rd | Chanh Tam pham | Tong Tam pham | **Tham tri** (Vice-Minister), **Tong doc** (Governor-General) | Brigade Commander |
| 4th | Chanh Tu pham | Tong Tu pham | **Tuan phu** (Provincial Governor), **Han lam** (Academician) | Garrison Commander |
| 5th | Chanh Ngu pham | Tong Ngu pham | **Tri phu** (Prefect) | District Military Officer |
| 6th | Chanh Luc pham | Tong Luc pham | **Tri huyen** (District Magistrate) | Company Commander |
| 7th | Chanh That pham | Tong That pham | Sub-district officials | Junior Military Officer |
| 8th | Chanh Bat pham | Tong Bat pham | Clerks, minor officials | Petty military officer |
| 9th | Chanh Cuu pham | Tong Cuu pham | Record-keepers, runners | Lowest military officer |

**Note on rank-to-position mapping:** The above mapping is approximate and based primarily on the Nguyen dynasty structure. Exact assignments varied by dynasty and by specific reign. Positions could carry a rank different from the holder's personal rank (pham ham).

### Key Administrative Positions

| Position | Role | Typical Rank |
|----------|------|-------------|
| **Thuong thu** (尚書) | Minister heading one of the Six Boards (Luc Bo) | 2nd rank |
| **Tong doc** (總督) | Governor-General overseeing 2-3 provinces | 2nd-3rd rank |
| **Tuan phu** (巡撫) | Provincial Governor | 3rd-4th rank |
| **Tham tri** (參知) | Vice-Minister | 3rd rank |
| **Bo chinh** (布政) | Provincial Administration Commissioner | 3rd-4th rank |
| **An sat** (按察) | Provincial Judicial Commissioner | 4th rank |
| **Tri phu** (知府) | Prefect | 5th rank |
| **Tri huyen** (知縣) | District Magistrate | 6th rank |
| **Han lam vien** (翰林院) | Hanlin Academy member (scholarly advisory body) | 4th-5th rank |

**The Six Boards (Luc Bo):** Lai (Personnel), Le (Rites), Ho (Revenue), Binh (Military), Hinh (Justice), Cong (Works). Each headed by a Thuong thu with a Tham tri as deputy.

### How Commoners Addressed Mandarins

- Mandarins were addressed as **Quan** (官) + title or **Quan lon** (great official)
- Specific forms: **Quan Huyen** (the District Magistrate), **Quan Phu** (the Prefect)
- Commoners referred to themselves as **dan** (subject/commoner) or **tieu nhan** (small person) when speaking to officials
- High mandarins might be called **Ngai** (a respectful "he/His Excellency")

## 3. Scholar Titles (Khoa cu)

### The Imperial Examination Ladder

The examination system was established in 1075 under the Ly dynasty and lasted until 1919. It had multiple levels:

**Regional Examinations (Thi Huong):**

| Title | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **Tu tai** (秀才) | "Cultivated Talent" -- passed the regional exam at the lowest level |
| **Cu nhan** (舉人) | "Recommended Person" -- passed the regional exam at the higher level |

**Metropolitan/Palace Examinations (Thi Hoi / Thi Dinh):**

| Title | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **Tien si** (進士) | "Advanced Scholar" -- passed the metropolitan exam. The standard doctoral degree. Title formalized in 1374 under Tran Due Tong. |
| **Pho bang** (副榜) | "Supplementary List" -- near-miss candidates given honorary recognition |

**Top Three (Tam Khoi) -- established 1247 under Tran dynasty:**

| Title | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **Trang nguyen** (狀元) | First-place scholar, the very top of the examination |
| **Bang nhan** (榜眼) | Second-place scholar ("Eyes of the List") |
| **Tham hoa** (探花) | Third-place scholar ("Flower Explorer") |

### How Scholars Were Addressed

- **Tu tai** and **Cu nhan** were addressed as **Thay** (teacher/master) by commoners, reflecting their learned status
- **Tien si** holders became mandarins and were addressed by their official rank
- **Trang nguyen** were celebrated nationally; addressed with great reverence, often simply as **Trang** or **Quan Trang**
- Scholars who did not hold office were still called **Thay** or **Ong** + given name
- The title **Do** (from Tien si = Do) was used colloquially: "Ong Do" referred to a scholar, especially one who practiced calligraphy

## 4. Commoner Honorifics in the Feudal Era

The Vietnamese pronoun/honorific system is kinship-based, and many terms used today originate from feudal-era usage. In the feudal context, these carried more rigid social weight:

| Term | Feudal-Era Usage |
|------|-----------------|
| **Cu** (祖) | Addressed very elderly persons (80+) or ancestors. Also a respectful term for high-ranking retired scholars or former officials. |
| **Ong** (翁) | Older man of standing; also used for grandfathers. In feudal context, implied a person of some social status (landowner, village elder). |
| **Ba** | Older woman of standing; female counterpart of Ong. |
| **Thay** (師) | Male teacher, scholar, or learned person. In feudal Vietnam, this was a mark of high respect -- scholars held elevated social status. Also used for Buddhist monks, medical practitioners, and fortune-tellers. |
| **Co** | Young woman or female teacher (Co giao). In feudal era, more strictly an unmarried young woman of good family. |
| **Anh** | Older brother or young man of slightly higher age. |
| **Chi** | Older sister or young woman of slightly higher age. |
| **Chu** | Uncle (father's younger brother) or a man somewhat older than the speaker. Also used to address mid-level officials informally. |
| **Bac** | Uncle (father's older brother) or a man significantly older than the speaker. More respectful than Chu. |

### Feudal-Era Social Hierarchy in Address

The "Four Pillars" (Tu Dan) of feudal Vietnamese society dictated forms of address:

1. **Si** (scholars) -- addressed as Thay, Ong, or by examination title
2. **Nong** (farmers) -- addressed by kinship terms based on age
3. **Cong** (artisans) -- addressed by kinship terms, sometimes with trade prefix
4. **Thuong** (merchants) -- lowest social class; addressed by kinship terms

Commoners addressing officials always used self-deprecating language and elevated terms for the official. The phrase "bam" or "thua" (to humbly report) preceded any statement to a superior.
