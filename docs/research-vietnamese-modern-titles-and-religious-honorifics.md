## 1. French Colonial Period (1858--1945) Titles

### The Dual Administrative System

French Indochina divided Vietnam into three territories, each with distinct governance and title systems:

| Territory | French Title | Vietnamese Title | Role |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|------|
| Cochinchina (Nam Ky) | Gouverneur | **Thong doc** | Governor -- direct French colony |
| Annam (Trung Ky) | Resident Superieur | **Kham su** | Resident Superior -- protectorate overseeing the Nguyen court |
| Tonkin (Bac Ky) | Resident Superieur | **Thong su** | Resident Superior -- protectorate |

The **Toan quyen** (Gouverneur-General) in Hanoi presided over all of Indochina (Vietnam, Laos, Cambodia).

### Retained Vietnamese Administrative Titles

The French preserved the Nguyen dynasty's provincial apparatus in Annam and Tonkin, creating a dual-title bureaucracy:

- **Tong doc** -- Governor-general of a pair of provinces (retained from Nguyen system)
- **Tuan phu** -- Provincial governor (subordinate to Tong doc)
- **Tri phu** -- Prefect (district-level administrator)
- **Tri huyen** -- Sub-prefect (county-level administrator)
- **Ly truong** -- Village chief (lowest formal administrative rank)

Vietnamese officials holding these posts served under French residents (*Cong su*) stationed at each province. The Vietnamese title carried traditional prestige but real authority lay with the French counterpart.

### Collaborator vs. Resistance Titles

Vietnamese who served the colonial administration were addressed by their Franco-Vietnamese titles (e.g., "Ong Tong doc"). Resistance figures rejected these titles entirely; anti-colonial movements used terms like **Chi huy** (commander), **Dong chi** (comrade, adopted from 1930 onward by communists), and traditional Vietnamese honorifics stripped of colonial association.

### Impact on Address Customs

The French period introduced **Ong/Ba** as universal formal address forms (paralleling Monsieur/Madame), displacing older status-specific terms. The French also established the convention of using professional titles as address forms -- a pattern that persists today (e.g., "Bac si Nguyen" for Dr. Nguyen).

## 2. Modern Professional/Academic Titles (Post-1945)

### Academic Titles

| Title | Equivalent | Usage as Address |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Giao su** (GS) | Professor | "Giao su [given name]" |
| **Pho Giao su** (PGS) | Associate Professor | "Pho Giao su [given name]" |
| **Tien si** (TS) | Doctor (PhD) | "Tien si [given name]" |
| **Thac si** (ThS) | Master's degree holder | Rarely used as address |
| **Cu nhan** | Bachelor's degree holder | Not used as address |

Academic titles are commonly stacked: "GS.TS Nguyen Van A" (Prof. Dr. Nguyen Van A). This stacking is a distinctly Vietnamese convention.

### Medical Titles

- **Bac si** (BS) -- Medical doctor (most commonly used professional title in daily life)
- **Duoc si** -- Pharmacist
- **Nha si** -- Dentist
- **Y ta** -- Nurse (not typically used as an address title)

"Bac si" functions as both title and address form: "Bac si oi!" (Doctor!) is standard in clinical settings.

### Legal Titles

- **Luat su** -- Lawyer/Attorney (used as address: "Luat su [name]")
- **Tham phan** -- Judge
- **Cong to vien** -- Prosecutor
- **Kiem sat vien** -- Procurator

### Military Ranks (Vietnam People's Army)

**General officers (cap tuong):**

| Rank | English Equivalent |
|------|-------------------|
| **Dai tuong** | General (4 stars, highest rank) |
| **Thuong tuong** | Colonel General (3 stars) |
| **Trung tuong** | Lieutenant General (2 stars) |
| **Thieu tuong** | Major General (1 star) |

**Field officers (cap ta):**

| Rank | English Equivalent |
|------|-------------------|
| **Dai ta** | Colonel |
| **Thuong ta** | Lieutenant Colonel (senior) |
| **Trung ta** | Lieutenant Colonel |
| **Thieu ta** | Major |

**Company officers (cap uy):**

| Rank | English Equivalent |
|------|-------------------|
| **Dai uy** | Captain |
| **Thuong uy** | Senior Lieutenant |
| **Trung uy** | Lieutenant |
| **Thieu uy** | Second Lieutenant |

**Enlisted (ha si quan and chien si):** Thuong si, Trung si, Ha si, Binh nhat, Binh nhi.

### Police Ranks (Cong an Nhan dan)

The Vietnamese People's Police use the same rank titles as the military (Dai tuong through Binh nhi) with identical hierarchy. The key difference is insignia color: police use a red background with yellow trim, while the army uses green. The Minister of Public Security holds the rank of Dai tuong.

### Political Titles

| Title | Role | Used as Address? |
|-------|------|-----------------|
| **Chu tich** | President / Chairman | Yes: "Chu tich [name]" |
| **Thu tuong** | Prime Minister | Yes |
| **Bo truong** | Minister | Yes |
| **Thu truong** | Deputy Minister | Yes |
| **Bi thu** | Party Secretary | Yes: "Bi thu [name]" |
| **Tong Bi thu** | General Secretary of CPV | Yes |
| **Chu tich Quoc hoi** | Chairman of National Assembly | Yes |

### "Thay/Co" -- The Teacher Honorific

**Thay** (male teacher) and **Co** (female teacher) extend far beyond schools. Vietnamese address martial arts instructors, religious mentors, fortune tellers, and any respected mentor as Thay/Co. A doctor's teacher is "Thay." A lawyer's mentor is "Thay." The term implies a lifelong moral debt (*on nghia*), not merely a professional relationship.

## 3. Religious Titles

### Buddhism

Vietnamese Buddhism (primarily Mahayana) uses a seniority-based title system governed by the Vietnam Buddhist Sangha (Giao hoi Phat giao Viet Nam).

**Male monastics:**

| Title | Sanskrit | Requirement | Meaning |
|-------|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Hoa thuong** | Upadhyaya | 40+ years ordained, age 60+ | Preceptor/Grand Master |
| **Thuong toa** | Sthavira | 25+ years ordained, age 45+ | Elder |
| **Dai duc** | Bhadanta | Fully ordained (250 precepts), age 20+ | Venerable |
| **Ty kheo** | Bhikkhu | Fully ordained monk | Monk |
| **Sa di** | Sramanera | Novice (10 precepts) | Novice monk |

**Female monastics:**

| Title | Requirement |
|-------|-------------|
| **Ni su** | Senior nun (equivalent to Thuong toa) |
| **Su co** | Nun (general address) |
| **Ty kheo ni** | Fully ordained nun (348 precepts) |
| **Sa di ni** | Novice nun |

**Lay address forms:** Laypeople address monks as **"Thay"** (teacher/master) in casual settings, or by title ("Bach Dai duc," "Bach Thuong toa") in formal contexts. "Bach" is a respectful verb meaning "to address" used exclusively toward monastics.

### Catholicism

Vietnam has the fifth-largest Catholic population in Asia (~7 million). Vietnamese Catholicism developed its own honorific vocabulary:

| Title | English | Address Form |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| **Giao hoang** | Pope | "Duc Giao Hoang" |
| **Hong y** | Cardinal | "Duc Hong Y" |
| **Tong Giam muc** | Archbishop | "Duc Tong Giam Muc" |
| **Giam muc** | Bishop | "Duc Cha" |
| **Linh muc** | Priest | **"Cha"** (Father) |
| **Pho te** | Deacon | "Thay" |
| **Nu tu** | Nun/Sister | **"So"** (Sister) or "Di" |
| **Be tren** | Superior (of a religious order) | "Be Tren" |

**Key cultural note:** Vietnamese Catholics address their parish priest as **"Cha"** (father), which carries the same emotional weight as the familial term. The priest's given name follows: "Cha Giuse" (Father Joseph). **"Cha xu"** or **"Cha chanh xu"** specifically denotes the parish pastor. Seminarians and deacons are addressed as **"Thay"**.

**Source:** [Wikipedia: Catholic Church in Vietnam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholic_Church_in_Vietnam)

### Cao Dai

Founded in 1926 in Tay Ninh, Cao Dai synthesizes Buddhism, Taoism, and Confucianism with a Catholic-style hierarchical clergy. The religion divides authority between two bodies:

**Cuu Trung Dai (Executive Body):**

| Rank | Equivalent | Count |
|------|-----------|-------|
| **Giao tong** | Pope | 1 |
| **Chuong phap** | Legislative Cardinal | 3 (one per branch) |
| **Dau su** | Cardinal | 3 |
| **Phoi su** | Archbishop | 36 |
| **Giao su** | Bishop | 72 |
| **Giao huu** | Priest | 3,000 |
| **Le sanh** | Student Priest | Unlimited |

**Hiep Thien Dai (Legislative/Judicial Body):**

| Rank | Role |
|------|------|
| **Ho phap** | Protector of Laws (head) |
| **Thuong pham** | Senior dignitary |
| **Thuong sanh** | Senior dignitary |

The three branches (Confucianist, Buddhist, Taoist) each have distinct vestment colors: red, saffron, and azure respectively. Each branch fills its own quota of clergy ranks.

**Source:** [Wikipedia: Caodaism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caodaism); [Cao Dai Center: Organization Structure](https://www.caodaicenter.org/organization-structure-3-5)

### Hoa Hao Buddhism

Founded in 1939 by Huynh Phu So in the Mekong Delta, Hoa Hao Buddhism deliberately rejects elaborate clerical hierarchy. It emphasizes lay practice over monastic institutions.

- The founder is called **Duc Huynh Giao Chu** (Venerable Founder Huynh)
- Followers address community leaders as **Ong** (Mr.) or **Bac** (elder) -- no special religious titles
- The organization uses administrative titles: **Hoi truong** (Chairman), **Tri su** (Administrator) at provincial/district levels
- This egalitarian address system reflects the religion's reform of Buddhism away from clerical hierarchy

**Source:** [Wikipedia: Hoa Hao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H%C3%B2a_H%E1%BA%A3o); [The Vietnamese Magazine: Hoa Hao Buddhism](https://thevietnamese.org/2021/08/the-tumultuous-and-tragic-history-of-hoa-hao-buddhism/)

### Folk Religion

Vietnamese folk religion has its own practitioners with distinct titles:

- **Thay cung** -- Ritual master (performs ancestor worship ceremonies, funerals, house blessings)
- **Thay phap** -- Taoist sorcerer/exorcist (performs spiritual protection rituals)
- **Ba dong** -- Female spirit medium (leads *hau dong* / *len dong* trance ceremonies)
- **Ong dong** -- Male spirit medium (less common)
- **Thay boi** -- Fortune teller / diviner

All are addressed as **Thay** (master) or **Ba** (madam) in practice.

## 4. Communist/Socialist Era Honorifics

### "Dong chi" (Comrade)

**Dong chi** (meaning "same aspiration") is the standard form of address among Communist Party of Vietnam members. Its usage follows specific rules:

- **Within Party meetings and documents:** Mandatory. All party members address each other as "dong chi" regardless of rank. Official CPV communications consistently use "Dong chi [full name]."
- **In government settings:** Used among party members; non-party civil servants are addressed by professional title or Ong/Ba.
- **In daily life:** Rare outside official contexts. Ordinary Vietnamese do not use "dong chi" in casual conversation. Its use signals a Party context.
- **Military/police:** "Dong chi" is used alongside military rank. A soldier may be addressed as "Dong chi Trung uy" (Comrade Lieutenant).

### "Bac Ho" -- The Unique Honorific for Ho Chi Minh

Ho Chi Minh is uniquely addressed as **"Bac"** (Uncle) -- specifically **"Bac Ho"** (Uncle Ho). This is not a generic honorific but a singular national convention:

- Originated during the resistance era when Ho Chi Minh presented himself as a fatherly/avuncular figure to the nation
- "Bac" places Ho Chi Minh in the kinship system as a respected elder uncle -- intimate yet authoritative
- No other Vietnamese leader is addressed as "Bac" in this manner
- The term carries deep emotional resonance; it humanizes national leadership in a way that "Chu tich" (Chairman) does not
- Official Vietnamese media consistently use "Bac Ho" alongside his formal title "Chu tich Ho Chi Minh"

### Party Titles as Address Forms

In Vietnamese political culture, Party titles function as primary address forms, often superseding government titles:

- **Tong Bi thu** (General Secretary) -- the most powerful title; used as direct address
- **Bi thu Tinh uy / Thanh uy** (Provincial/City Party Secretary) -- addressed as "Bi thu"
- **Bi thu Dang uy** (Party Committee Secretary) -- at organizational level
- Party rank often takes precedence over state rank when both apply to the same person
