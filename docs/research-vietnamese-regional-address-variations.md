# Regional Variations in Vietnamese Address Terms, Honorifics, and Pronouns

> Research document for `vietnamese-name-generator` library -- region-aware salutation generation.
> Last updated: 2026-04-06

---

## 1. Northern Vietnam (Mien Bac)

### 1.1 Ha Noi and Red River Delta

**Pronoun usage.** Hanoi speakers favor the pair **to-cau** among close friends and peers, a characteristic of the capital's speech that is less common elsewhere in the North. **Toi** remains the default formal first-person pronoun and is more frequently heard in Hanoi than in Southern cities, reflecting the Northern preference for measured formality. In professional or governmental settings, **toi** paired with kinship honorifics (anh/chi/ong/ba) is standard.

**Addressing strangers.** Hanoians tend to default to **bac** (father's older sibling) when addressing an unfamiliar person who appears older than one's parents, rather than **chu/co** (father's younger siblings). This upward-aging bias reflects the cultural principle of erring toward greater respect. For peers, **anh/chi** is used generously even when the age difference is uncertain.

**"Ha Noi thanh lich" (elegant Hanoi).** The phrase captures a speech culture that prizes indirectness, softened requests, and elaborate honorific chains. Formal Hanoi speech uses **Kinh thua** (respectfully addressing) and **Thua** (respectfully) as sentence-initial markers far more frequently than Southern speech. The polite affirmative is **Vang** (yes), distinct from the Southern **Da**. Hanoi speakers historically avoid blunt pronoun pairs like **tao-may** in public, reserving them for genuinely intimate or confrontational contexts.

**Key terms:**

| Term | Usage |
|------|-------|
| To-Cau | Peer pronoun pair (Hanoi characteristic, casual-intimate) |
| Toi | Formal first person (default in professional settings) |
| Bac | Respectful default for older strangers |
| Vang | Polite affirmative response (Northern) |
| Kinh thua | Formal opening honorific phrase |

### 1.2 Northeast and Northwest Highlands

**Ethnic minority address influences.** The Northern highlands are home to the **Tay**, **Nung**, **Hmong**, and **Dao** peoples, whose languages belong to the Tai-Kadai, Hmong-Mien, and other non-Vietic families. In mixed communities, Vietnamese kinship terms (anh, chi, chu, co) are used in Vietnamese conversation, but minority-language address forms persist in bilingual households.

**The "A" prefix.** In Hmong and some Tai-speaking communities, given names are frequently prefixed with **"A"** as a familiar address marker: **A Phu**, **A Su**, **A Chang**. This prefix (from the Chinese/Tai "a" vocative particle) signals intimacy or informal address. It appears in Vietnamese literature depicting highland life (e.g., To Hoai's "Vo chong A Phu"). When Kinh (ethnic Vietnamese) speakers in highland areas adopt local customs, they may use the "A" prefix in mixed-language conversation.

**Mixed address patterns in border areas.** In provinces like Cao Bang, Lang Son, and Ha Giang, Tay-Nung bilingual speakers frequently substitute Tai kinship terms -- **lung** (uncle), **pa** (father), **me** (mother in Tay) -- in otherwise Vietnamese sentences. This code-switching is common in market settings and informal cross-ethnic interactions.

## 2. Central Vietnam (Mien Trung)

### 2.1 Hue and Thua Thien

Hue Vietnamese preserves the most distinctive regional address system in the country, with terms that differ sharply from both Northern and Southern usage.

**Core kinship divergences:**

| Hue term | Standard equivalent | Meaning |
|----------|-------------------|---------|
| **Cau** | Ba/Cha | Father (unique to Hue; elsewhere "cau" means maternal uncle) |
| **Ma** | Me | Mother |
| **On** | Ong noi/ngoai | Grandfather |
| **Me** (me) | Ba noi/ngoai | Grandmother |
| **O** | Co/Chi | Young woman, aunt (paternal) |
| **Mu** | Thi/Ba | Older woman, aunt by marriage |

**The mi-tau pair.** Hue speakers use **mi** (you) and **tau** (I) as an intimate pronoun pair among close friends or between spouses in private. Outside Hue, this pair is widely perceived as rude or vulgar -- a significant pragmatic mismatch that causes cross-regional misunderstandings. In Hue itself, mi-tau carries warmth and closeness.

**Elaborate formality.** Hue speech historically employs more honorific layers than either Northern or Southern Vietnamese, a legacy of the city's status as imperial capital (1802--1945). Formal Hue speech uses **Bam** (to humbly report to a superior), **Thuong toa** (Your Excellency, Buddhist), and extended deferential phrasing. The tone of address tends toward indirectness and verbal humility.

**Royal/aristocratic address remnants.** Families descended from the Nguyen dynasty use the compound surnames **Ton That** (male line) and **Ton Nu** (female line) as markers of imperial lineage. These surnames, derived from Sino-Vietnamese "Tong That" (clan members), were changed under Emperor Thieu Tri due to naming taboo. In modern Hue, these families may still use internal address forms that reflect aristocratic hierarchy, though this is fading with each generation.

### 2.2 Nghe An -- Ha Tinh (Nghe Tinh)

The Nghe Tinh dialect is considered one of the most distinctive and difficult-to-comprehend Vietnamese dialects. Its kinship terms diverge significantly from standard Vietnamese.

**Key address term differences:**

| Nghe Tinh term | Standard equivalent | Notes |
|----------------|-------------------|-------|
| **Bo** | Ba/Cha | Father |
| **Me/Ma** | Me | Mother (phonetic shift) |
| **Eng** | Anh | Older brother (consonant shift anh -> eng) |
| **A** | Chi | Older sister (replaces chi entirely) |
| **O** | Co | Aunt (paternal), young woman |
| **Cau** | Cau | Maternal uncle (same as standard) |

**Pronoun pairs.** Nghe Tinh speakers use **tau-mi** similarly to Hue but with different tonal realization due to the dialect's reduced tone system (4-5 tones vs. standard 6). The perception of Nghe Tinh accent in address is often associated with directness, toughness, and rural authenticity -- stereotypes that can affect how speakers are received in Hanoi or Saigon.

**Phonetic shifts affecting address.** The Nghe Tinh dialect merges or transforms tones (acute/underdot, hook/tilde), causing standard address terms to sound markedly different: **anh** becomes **eng**, **chi** becomes something closer to **a**, and **cha/ba** shifts to **bo**. These are not slang but systematic phonological changes.

### 2.3 Quang Nam -- Da Nang

Quang Nam-Da Nang speech sits at the transition zone between Hue formality and Southern casualness. Speakers use some Hue-origin terms (**o** for young woman, **ma** for mother) but with less elaborate honorific layering. The pronoun system leans toward Hue patterns (mi-tau among intimates) but speakers adopt Southern-style directness in commercial settings. Da Nang, as a major city, increasingly blends standard (Hanoi-broadcast) Vietnamese with local Central forms.

## 3. Southern Vietnam (Mien Nam)

### 3.1 Sai Gon and Dong Nam Bo (Southeast)

**First-person pronoun.** The default casual first person is **tui** (derived from toi but with Southern tonal/vowel shift), used across all social classes in informal speech. This contrasts with the Northern preference for the full **toi** even in semi-casual contexts.

**Parental address:**

| Southern term | Standard/Northern | Origin |
|--------------|-------------------|--------|
| **Ma** | Me | Mother |
| **Ba** | Bo/Cha | Father (standard in South) |
| **Tia** | Bo/Cha | Father (Teochew origin, more common in Mekong) |

**"Da" vs "Vang".** The polite affirmative response in the South is **Da** (with falling tone), while the North uses **Vang**. This is one of the most immediately identifiable North-South markers. Southern children are taught to say "Da" to elders; Northern children say "Vang." Using the wrong one marks a speaker as an outsider.

**Chinese-Vietnamese (Hoa) influence.** The Teochew (Trieu Chau) community, comprising roughly 30% of ethnic Chinese in Vietnam and concentrated in Saigon-Cholon and the Mekong Delta, contributed several address terms to Southern Vietnamese:
- **Che** -- older sister (from Teochew "che/chia"), used widely in Saigon, especially among women
- **Tia** -- father (from Teochew), common in Mekong Delta
- **Hia** -- older brother (from Hokkien/Teochew "hia"), used in Chinese-Vietnamese families

**Birth-order addressing.** Southern families commonly address members by birth order: the firstborn is **Hai** (two -- the count starts at two, skipping "Mot/Nhat"), the second child is **Ba** (three), etc. This yields address forms like **Anh Hai**, **Chi Ba**, **Chu Tu**. This system is especially prevalent in the South; in the North, birth-order names begin with **Ca** (eldest) rather than Hai.

**Casual default formality.** Southern Vietnamese defaults to a lower formality register than Northern Vietnamese. Strangers may be addressed with **anh/chi** rather than **ong/ba** even when the age gap might warrant the more formal term in the North.

### 3.2 Tay Nam Bo (Southwest / Mekong Delta)

The Mekong Delta has the most distinctive Southern address vocabulary, with heavy Teochew, Khmer, and rural Vietnamese influences.

**Key Mekong-specific terms:**

| Term | Meaning | Notes |
|------|---------|-------|
| **Tia** | Father | Teochew origin; dominant in rural Mekong |
| **Ma / Ba Gia** | Mother | "Ba Gia" (lit. old lady) is affectionate, not pejorative |
| **Ut** | Youngest child | Used as both name and address term |
| **Bay** | Plural "you" | Unique to Mekong; addresses a group informally |
| **Qua** | I/me (older to younger) | Self-reference by an older person speaking to someone younger; Mekong-specific |
| **Ngoai** | Maternal grandparent | Shortened from "Ong/Ba Ngoai" |

**Birth-order naming/addressing.** The Mekong Delta carries birth-order addressing to its fullest extent: **Hai, Ba, Tu, Nam, Sau, Bay, Tam, Chin, Muoi** (2 through 10). People may be known their entire lives by their birth-order name rather than their given name. A man called "Sau" throughout the village may have "Sau" function as his primary social identifier.

**"Qua" as self-reference.** In Mekong speech, an older person speaking to a younger person may say **qua** (I/me) instead of standard kinship self-references. This is a distinctively southwestern form rarely heard in Saigon proper or the North. Example: "Qua noi cho bay nghe" (Let me tell you all something).

**Khmer influence.** In provinces with significant Khmer Krom populations (Tra Vinh, Soc Trang, An Giang), Khmer address terms sometimes appear in local Vietnamese: **lok** (Mr./respected person), **neang** (young woman). Khmer-Vietnamese bilingual families may code-switch between Vietnamese kinship terms and Khmer equivalents depending on context. The Khmer practice of using religious titles (Achar, Lok Kru) for community elders also influences local Vietnamese address customs.

### 3.3 Tay Nguyen (Central Highlands)

**Ethnic minority address systems.** The Central Highlands are home to the **E-de**, **Gia Rai** (Jarai), **Ba Na**, and other Austronesian and Mon-Khmer speaking peoples whose address systems differ fundamentally from Vietnamese.

**E-de (Rhade) terms:**

| E-de term | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| **Ama** | Father |
| **Ami** | Mother |
| **Adue** | Younger sibling |
| **Ayong** | Older sibling |

**Matrilineal naming influence.** The E-de follow a matrilineal system where lineage passes through the mother. This affects address: a child takes the mother's clan name, and maternal relatives hold higher address priority than paternal ones. When E-de speakers use Vietnamese, they may map their matrilineal address hierarchy onto Vietnamese kinship terms, sometimes producing address patterns that seem unusual to Kinh speakers (e.g., prioritizing the mother's brother over the father's brother in honorific ranking).

**Gia Rai and Ba Na.** These groups use similar parent terms (Ama/Ami variants) and have clan-based address systems where one's clan affiliation determines the appropriate terms for non-relatives. In Vietnamese-medium schools and government settings, these speakers adopt standard Vietnamese address (thay/co for teachers, anh/chi for peers) but revert to indigenous terms in family and village contexts.

## Key Implications for Library Implementation

1. **The `ERegion` enum (North/Central/South) maps well** to the three major address systems, though Central should ideally distinguish Hue from Nghe Tinh.
2. **The current `salutation.ts` is region-agnostic** -- it uses only standard Ong/Ba/Anh/Chi. Adding region-aware honorifics (O, Che, Cau-as-father) would significantly improve cultural accuracy.
3. **The "Da" vs "Vang" distinction** is a high-impact, low-effort addition for region-aware polite responses.
4. **Birth-order addressing** (Hai, Ba, Tu...) is primarily Southern and could be generated alongside names when `region: 'south'`.
5. **Ethnic minority terms** (Ama/Ami, A-prefix) represent a stretch goal requiring a sub-region or ethnicity parameter.
